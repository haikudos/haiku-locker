§ 01 / orientation

The threat sits in three layers — settings alone fix one of them.

If you only harden the account layer, you have solved roughly a third of the problem. The session layer bypasses your 2FA entirely. The human layer bypasses your software entirely. Every real compromise we have on record touched layer two or three.

FIG. 01 — Threat layer stack read top-down ↓
Read this carefully. The session layer (tdata, auth tokens) bypasses the account layer entirely — no password, no SMS, no 2FA. An attacker who copies your tdata folder is logged in as you on their machine. Your 2-step verification password never gets challenged.

Most generic "Telegram security" advice you find online is layer 01 advice. Turn on 2FA, hide your phone number, use Secret Chats. That advice is necessary but completely insufficient for crypto operators. The actors targeting your industry — DPRK-linked clusters like UNC1069 / Lazarus, Iranian groups like Handala, plus a global commodity-stealer market — long ago moved on from credential phishing to session theft and trusted-contact takeover.

! Why your industry, specifically

Your Telegram contact list is the asset. Crypto journalists, founders, and treasury operators are targeted not always for their own funds, but because their compromised account lets attackers send messages to dozens of other high-value contacts under a trusted name. The first victim is a stepping stone.

§ 03 / clickfix

The ClickFix attack — single most common script today.

If your team learns nothing else, they need to recognize this one pattern. It is responsible for a majority of recent crypto-industry compromises tracked by Mandiant and SEAL 911.

FIG. 03 — ClickFix kill-chain ~7–12 min from intro to ownership
The whole trick is the script. Everything before it — the intro, the Zoom, the audio bug — exists only to make pasting a command into Terminal feel normal. There is no legitimate scenario where a video call's audio problem is fixed by running a script in Terminal. Ever. The rule for your team is binary: commands from chat → never run, no exceptions, no troubleshooting.
! Deepfakes are not authentication

Since November 2025, Mandiant has tracked AI-generated deepfake video deployed inside Zoom meetings by UNC1069. Seeing a face you recognize on a call no longer proves identity. Voice cloning is also commodity. The only authentication that still works is out-of-band verification on a channel you initiated (a phone call to a number you already had).

§ 04 / session theft

The tdata problem — why 2FA does not save you.

Telegram Desktop keeps you logged in by storing your session in a folder called tdata. Whoever has that folder is you. No password challenge. No SMS. No 2FA prompt. This is the single most under-appreciated vulnerability in the entire ecosystem.

FIG. 04 — How tdata theft works no auth challenge required
Documented delivery vectors for tdata theft in the last 18 months: malicious PyPI packages (typosquats like quicolor, QuickColors, ColorYi targeting quickcolor), malicious npm packages, fake "update" installers shared via Telegram channels, the ClickFix Terminal/PowerShell scripts described in §03, PowerShell scripts hosted on Pastebin, and Linux honeypot incidents starting from weak SSH credentials. The commodity stealer market is large, cheap, and increasingly Telegram-aware.

+ Defenses that work against tdata theft

  • Treat the workstation running Telegram Desktop as a high-value endpoint. Full-disk encryption, EDR, locked-down user account.
  • For treasury / signing / admin roles: do not use Telegram Desktop at all. Mobile only.
  • Audit active sessions every 2 weeks. Terminate anything you don't recognize immediately, even if it's labeled normally.
  • Block api.telegram.org from non-Telegram processes via EDR rules where possible.
  • Alert on any non-Telegram process touching \Telegram Desktop\tdata\.

× What 2FA / passwords cannot fix

  • 2-step verification password is never prompted when restoring a session from tdata. It does nothing here.
  • Changing your Telegram password does not invalidate already-stolen sessions. You must explicitly Terminate All Other Sessions.
  • SMS code rotation does nothing. The session bypasses the SMS layer.
  • Secret Chats do not protect you — once the attacker is logged in, they see everything that isn't a device-specific secret chat.
  • Recovery email does not help. The session is already authenticated.
§ 05 / login codes

The six-digit code is a master key. Treat it like one.

Telegram authentication leans heavily on the SMS / in-app login code. Most account takeovers that do not involve tdata theft involve someone obtaining this code — by tricking the user, by hijacking the phone number, or by intercepting it at the network level.

FIG. 05 — Five OTP attack paths (Handala / Iranian MOIS pattern) all observed in 2025–2026
The two defenses that close most of these: (1) 2-Step Verification password — even with the SMS code, an attacker also needs this password. Enable it. (2) Never share the code or scan QR codes from any source other than the Telegram app on a device you control. Telegram support will never DM you asking for it. No one "sends a code by mistake." The code is a master key, not a piece of casual information.
! Real-world reference

In December 2025, the Iranian-linked group Handala compromised Telegram accounts of two prominent Israeli political figures. Initial reporting claimed full phone compromise; forensic analysis showed it was Telegram-account-only — pure OTP harvesting plus desktop session hijacking. The devices were never touched. The attack happened entirely through the layers above.

§ 06 / defaults

Telegram defaults are not built for you.

Telegram defaults optimize for ease of finding people, cross-device sync, and casual social use. Almost every default is wrong for an operator at a crypto company. You need to walk through every setting once and change roughly fifteen of them.

▲ A note on Secret Chats

Regular chats on Telegram are not end-to-end encrypted. They are encrypted in transit and at rest, but Telegram holds the keys. Only Secret Chats are end-to-end encrypted, and they are device-specific (do not sync). For any 1:1 conversation that contains genuinely sensitive content, use Secret Chat with a self-destruct timer, on mobile. Note that Telegram Web and Telegram Desktop on Windows do not support Secret Chats reliably.

§ 07 / hardening

The checklist — give this to every employee, day one.

Print this. Walk through it on each person's device with them. Do not assume someone will do it on their own. Verify completion.

  • 2-Step Verification — set in Settings → Privacy and Security. Strong unique password. Recovery email on a separately secured mailbox (not your daily Gmail).
  • Phone number visibility — "Who can see my phone number" set to Nobody. "Who can find me by my number" set to My Contacts (or Nobody for treasury / admins).
  • Last Seen & Online — Nobody or My Contacts.
  • Profile photo — My Contacts.
  • Calls — My Contacts. Peer-to-Peer — Nobody.
  • Forwarded Messages — link to my account: My Contacts or Nobody.
  • Groups & Channels — who can add me: My Contacts.
  • Passcode Lock / Face ID — enabled on every device. 6-digit minimum, auto-lock 1 minute.
  • Data Settings → Sync Contacts — OFF. Then tap Delete Synced Contacts to remove what's already uploaded.
  • Active Sessions audit — list every device, terminate anything unrecognized, schedule a recurring biweekly review.
  • Desktop client decision — for treasury / signing / channel-admin roles, uninstall Telegram Desktop entirely. Mobile only.
  • For users keeping Desktop — confirm full-disk encryption is on, EDR is running, non-admin daily user account, no portable / unofficial Telegram builds.
  • Bot audit — remove every bot from your DMs that you are not actively using.
  • Auto-delete — enabled on sensitive 1:1 chats with a 1-week default.
  • Mental rule installed — "I will never paste a command from a chat into Terminal, PowerShell, or cmd. Not for audio, not for video, not to install anything. Ever."
§ 08 / channel governance

The cold-owner model — separate ownership from operations.

Your instinct here is correct and matches how serious crypto operations run channel security. The owner account is a vault key. The admin accounts are the daily-drivers. They are not the same account. They never sit on the same device. The owner account is offline almost all the time.

▲ Why this matters, concretely

A real February 2026 case: a project lead's account was hijacked via a compromised browser extension. Before he realized, the attacker had transferred ownership of three Telegram groups including the main community channel, rewritten the description to a phishing link disguised as a "token migration portal," and drained hundreds of thousands of dollars from members within hours. The single point of failure was that the daily-driver account was also the channel owner. The cold-owner model eliminates that link.

FIG. 07 — Cold-owner architecture recommended deployment
The trade-off you are making: a small amount of operational friction (admins post, owner approves rotations monthly) in exchange for converting a catastrophic event ("channel ownership transferred + audience phished") into a recoverable one ("rogue admin posts for a few hours until removed"). The blast radius collapses from channel-level to post-level.

The setup, step by step

  1. Provision a dedicated phone number for the owner account. eSIM on a hardened device is acceptable. Do not use a number that belongs to any specific employee's personal life — when they leave, you don't want to lose the channel. Treat the number as company property, port-locked at the carrier with a strong custom PIN. A non-VoIP physical SIM is preferred (Telegram has been known to restrict some VoIP numbers, and SS7-based interception is a known vector for many VoIP providers).
  2. Register the owner account on a dedicated device. A separate phone (or a clean profile on a hardware-isolated device) that is not used for anything else. No personal apps, no email, no browsing. This device's only job is to occasionally launch Telegram. Keep it locked in the office safe between uses.
  3. On first login, enable 2-Step Verification with a long random password stored in a shared password manager vault (1Password Business, Bitwarden, etc.) accessible to the security team only. Set a recovery email on a separately-hardened mailbox — ideally a Google Workspace account with hardware-key 2FA enabled and dedicated to operations.
  4. Create the channels / supergroups from this account. Configure them. Then promote your operational admins with granular permissions.
  5. Use granular admin permissions, not blanket "Admin" rights. Telegram lets you toggle each capability individually: Send Messages, Edit Messages of Others, Delete Messages of Others, Ban Users, Invite Users, Pin Messages, Manage Video Chats, Remain Anonymous, Add New Admins. Crucially: leave "Add New Admins" OFF for every admin. Only the cold owner can add admins. This breaks the chain where one compromised admin escalates to giving themselves more privileges or recruiting an attacker account.
  6. Enable "Remain Anonymous" for admin posting in your main channels. This is a Telegram feature where admin actions show as posted by the channel itself, not by the admin's personal account. Benefit: your individual admins are not enumerable to attackers scanning your channel for high-value impersonation targets. Their personal Telegram accounts become invisible from the channel context.
  7. Log out of the owner account from the device after setup. Do not leave it running. Do not stay logged in for convenience. Every time you finish an owner-session, terminate the session from inside Telegram before closing the app, then physically lock the device away.
  8. Schedule a recurring owner-session. Monthly is the floor; biweekly is better. The checklist for each session: (1) review every admin's continued employment and need, (2) remove anyone who has left the team or no longer needs the role, (3) check Active Sessions on the owner account — there should be exactly zero, terminate anything else with extreme prejudice, (4) confirm 2FA password is unchanged, (5) review channel description / pinned message / linked group for any unexpected changes, (6) log out.

Specific Telegram mechanics you should know

Two of these (A and B) are the most often-missed. The 7-day + 1-month transfer guard means you cannot rotate the owner on short notice — provision the backup owner account well before you need it. The deletion-destroys-ownership rule means inactivity on the cold owner is itself a risk; mark the calendar.

Refinements to your stated plan

You said: "hardened account that is the channel owner and only used to hire some admins. Not have that account with any active sessions except for channel creation." This is right. Two things I would add:

  • Pair the cold owner with a "backup owner" account on identical principles — same vault, same level of hardening, different number and device — and pre-warm it past the 7-day / 1-month transfer guard. If the primary owner is ever lost (device destroyed, number ported, account locked), you can transfer ownership to the backup in minutes instead of begging Telegram support for weeks.
  • Schedule a quarterly "proof-of-life" login on the cold owner. Not because you need to do anything — just to keep Telegram's inactivity counters from drifting toward auto-deletion. Set a calendar reminder for the security officer. Five minutes on the device, log in, verify Active Sessions = 1, log out.
  • Document the recovery procedure on paper or in your incident-response wiki: what number, what 2FA password vault entry, what recovery email, what device location, who has access. Test the procedure once a year. A cold owner you cannot recover is worse than no cold owner at all.
  • Treat the cold-owner number as a port-out target. Set carrier port-out protection with a custom PIN. Subscribe the line to carrier breach alerts. A SIM swap on this number bypasses your entire model.
  • Keep the cold owner out of any DMs. It has no contact list, no inbound messages, no group memberships beyond the channels it owns. This minimizes the social-engineering attack surface — there is no one to impersonate, no one to phish, no one to send a ClickFix script.
! One thing to think hard about

The cold-owner model raises the bar against external attackers significantly. It does not protect against insider risk — whoever has the vault credentials for the cold owner has full control of every channel. Limit that access list aggressively (security officer + one backup, no one else), audit the vault for who has viewed the entry, and rotate the 2FA password if anyone with access leaves the company. This becomes one of the most sensitive credentials in your entire organization. Treat it accordingly.

§ 09 / verification protocol

The out-of-band verification protocol.

This is the answer to the human layer. Every documented high-value Telegram compromise in crypto worked because the victim trusted who they were talking to. The fix is structural: a small set of triggers that force you off Telegram to verify identity before acting.

FIG. 06 — Decision tree apply on every inbound DM
One rule, internalized. If a message contains a link, a file, a script, an intro to a third party, anything about money/signing/approval, or anything that pressures urgency — you stop and verify on a channel that does not run through Telegram. The cost is 60 seconds of friction per suspect message. The benefit is the only working defense against trusted-contact takeover.

Mandatory verification triggers

  • Any request to move to Zoom, Google Meet, or another video platform with someone you have not met there before.
  • Any request to download anything, run a script, install a "plugin," or troubleshoot audio/video by running commands.
  • Any introduction to a third party ("meet my friend who…", "I want you to talk to…").
  • Any request involving funds, signing, approval, credentials, or wallet addresses, regardless of urgency or who appears to be asking.
  • Any link to a "verification", "KYC", "airdrop", "claim", or "support" portal.
  • Any private message from a group admin of a project you are in — admins almost never DM unsolicited.
  • Any message whose tone or timing feels off, even slightly. Trust the instinct.
§ 10 / incident response

If you suspect compromise — act in this order.

Speed matters. The longer an attacker is logged into your account, the more contacts they message and the more secondary compromises they cause. The first ten minutes are the most important.

  1. From a known-clean device (a phone you trust, not the suspect machine), open Telegram → Settings → Devices → Terminate All Other Sessions. This is the single most important action and must happen first. Even if you change passwords, existing sessions remain valid until terminated.
  2. Change Two-Step Verification password immediately afterward. Set a new strong one. Confirm recovery email is intact and accessible.
  3. If Telegram Desktop was running on a suspect machine: assume the entire host is compromised. Disconnect it from the network. Do not just run an antivirus scan. You will need to wipe and reimage.
  4. Rotate every credential that was ever entered or stored on that machine: browser passwords (every saved password, full export from password manager and replace), SSH keys, cloud API tokens, GitHub PATs, AWS / GCP keys, wallet seed phrases if any were entered or stored on disk, exchange API keys, anything in ~/.aws/, ~/.ssh/, password manager vault password.
  5. Notify contacts via a non-Telegram channel — email, Slack, Signal — that your account may have been used to send malicious messages during a defined window (e.g., "any DM from me between 14:00 and 18:00 today should be ignored"). Be specific about the window to maintain credibility.
  6. Preserve evidence before deleting. Screenshot or save suspicious DMs, attacker phone numbers, links, and downloaded file hashes. Report to SEAL 911 (or your internal security team) so IoCs can be shared with the broader crypto security community.
  7. On-chain audit. For 30 days back, audit every wallet that interacted with anything received via Telegram — addresses, signed transactions, approvals. If anything is suspicious, revoke approvals and consider moving funds.
  8. Post-mortem. Document exactly how it happened. Share internally — without blame — so the team learns the specific lure. The next attack on your team will probably look similar.
§ 11 / hard rules

What Telegram is not for.

These are the lines you do not cross, even if it's "just this once," even if it's "from the founder," even if it's urgent. The exceptions are what attackers exploit.

× Never on Telegram

  • Seed phrases, private keys, mnemonic recovery words
  • API keys, signing keys, password manager exports
  • Multisig coordination, signing approvals, transaction hashes-to-sign
  • Treasury operations (transfers, swaps, large approvals)
  • 2FA codes for any service, including Telegram itself
  • Sensitive documents (term sheets, legal contracts, payroll, KYC documents)
  • Use as a primary 2FA destination for other services
  • Running any command pasted from a chat in Terminal / PowerShell / cmd

+ Use Telegram for

  • Community engagement (with two-admin posting discipline)
  • Conference logistics, casual coordination
  • Public announcements (channel post, reviewed by a second admin)
  • Casual conversations where compromise would be embarrassing at worst, not catastrophic
  • Anything you would be willing to publish on a billboard
! The principle

Assume every Telegram chat may, on any given day, become attacker-readable. If that assumption changes your behavior, the content does not belong in Telegram. There is no "secure enough" configuration that justifies keeping the things in the left column on this platform.
